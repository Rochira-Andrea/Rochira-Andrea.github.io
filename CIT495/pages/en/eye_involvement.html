Eye Involvement

When the blood vessels overgrowth involves the eyes, glaucoma is the most common diagnosis. Glaucoma is an eye condition related to the damage of the optic nerve due to intraocular high pressure, which may result in blindness or vision loss if left untreated.
Eye pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mm Hg). Normal eye pressure ranges from 12-22 mm Hg, therefore, any value greater than 22 mm Hg is considered high.

The optic nerve is a bundle of more than 1 million nerve fibers. It connects the retina to the brain. The retina is the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye. A healthy optic nerve is necessary for good vision.
(National Eye Institute)

A glaucoma can be diagnosed right after the birth, even when still at the hospital, thus receiving medical attention within the first week of life. Typically, the white portion of the eye appears "bloodshot" as a result of the over-proliferation of blood vessels. Usually in the beginning there are no symptoms, but it’s strongly recommended to always check on the affected eye as soon as possible.

Statistically, if one eye suffers from glaucoma, there’s a 30% chance that the other eye can be affected too. On top of that, when the port wine stain affects eye lids, there’s a lifelong high chance that the eye can develop a glaucoma. Therefore is highly recommended to perform regular ophthalmic checks on it.

Types of glaucoma

There are numerous types of glaucoma, yet the most common two are open-angle and angle closure glaucoma. For newborns with glaucoma at birth, it is commonly known as congenital glaucoma.
Here is a list of most known:

  • Normal Tension glaucoma (NTG), also called low-tension or normal-pressure glaucoma
  • Secondary glaucoma
  • Pigmentary glaucoma
  • Pseudo-exfoliative glaucoma
  • Traumatic glaucoma
  • Neovascular glaucoma
  • Irido Corneal Endothelial Syndrome (ICE)
  • Uveitic glaucoma

Detection

It is very important to have regular eye examinations in order to detect it. It is usually noticed through a comprehensive dilated eye exam (drops placed in the patient’s eyes to widen, or dilate, the pupils) that includes the following:

  • Visual acuity test: An eye chart test to measure how well one sees at diverse distances.
  • Visual field test: Measures peripheral (side vision). It helps the eye care professional determine if there is loss of peripheral vision, which is a sign of glaucoma.
  • Tonometry: Measures pressure inside the eye by utilizing an instrument called a tonometer.
  • Pachymetry: Measurement the thickness of the cornea.

How to treat

Immediate and early intervention of glaucoma can delay progression of the disease. That’s why early diagnosis is so important. Treatments vary depending on the type of glaucoma.
In general, treatments include medicines, laser trabeculoplasty, conventional surgery, or a combination of these. People without treatment slowly lose their peripheral or side vision eventually having straight-ahead vision as if looking into a tunnel until there is no longer any vision at all. It is important to understand that pressure in the eye is reversible. What is not reversible is the damage of the optic nerve due to high pressure. In order to prevent complications, patients can recur to medication or surgery. Early intervention is the best way to protect eyes against vision loss.

  • Medicines

They come in the form of eyedrops or pills. They are the most common early treatment for glaucoma. Eyedrops lower eye pressure and are usually taken regularly. Another important factor is that because glaucoma often has no symptoms, patients might be tempted to stop taking, or may forget to take, their glaucoma medication. Drops or pills need to be taken as they help control the eye pressure. Regular use is very important!

  • Laser trabeculoplasty

This helps fluid drain out of the eye. Doctors may suggest this step at any time. In frequent cases, taking glaucoma medicines after this procedure must continue. If there is glaucoma in both eyes, usually only one eye will be treated at a time. In general, studies show that laser surgery can be very helpful at reducing the pressure in some patients. However, the effects can wear off over time and the doctors may suggest more treatment.

  • Trabeculectomy

Trabeculectomy belongs to the standard or conventional glaucoma surgery category and has been the main surgical procedure worldwide for almost 50 years. It is often performed after medicines and laser surgery have failed to control the eye pressure. This major surgical procedure makes a new opening for the fluids to leave the eye.
If performed early, it can give excellent intraocular pressure control (from 60 to 80 percent decrease) with minimal complications.

  • MIGS

It's the abbreviation of Micro or Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery and it describes a group of surgical procedures that work by using microscopic-sized equipment and tiny incisions. While they reduce the incidence of complications, some degree of effectiveness is also traded for the increased safety.
While MIGS procedures will not replace or eliminate conventional glaucoma surgery, they have greatly decreased the number of patients who require more invasive surgeries to control their intraocular pressure. MIGS can be thought of in a few broad categories, either enhancing fluid outflow using the eye’s inherent drainage system, shunting fluid to the outside of the eye or decreasing production of fluid within the eye.
In my son's case, a MIGS procedure known as Gonioscopy-Assisted Transluminal Trabeculotomy (GATT) was able to let the eye pressure drop from over 40 to lower than 25 mm Hg.
