Sturge-Weber Syndrome

Simply Put for Everyone

Let's start with a little bit of history. It's not uncommon that a medical condition takes its name from those who originally diagnosed it. Back in 1879, William Allen Sturge, English physician and pathologist, described a very peculiar disorder in a six-year-old child, subsequently recorded in the Transactions of the Clinical Society of London. Four decades later, Frederick Parkes Weber, an English dermatologist, was able to provide the first radiologic evidence of the same disorder. They were both observing and diagnosing the same condition, which is known today as the Sturge-Weber syndrome (abbreviated, SWS).

SWS is a rare condition characterized by some or all of the following symptoms:

  • a birthmark called port wine stain varying in size, diameter, distribution, and depth from one individual to another and even within the same person in different affected areas
  • eye irregularities (such as glaucoma, seen in up to 70% of cases)
  • atypical growth of blood vessels within the meninges or the thin layers of tissue covering the brain, from which derives the name leptomeningeal angioma (seen on one side of the brain in 85% of cases and on both sides of the brain in 15% of cases)

Another mouthful name for SWS is encephalo-trigeminal angiomatosis, which provides more insights if properly understood. Encephalo means "related to the brain", trigeminal defines the largest and most complex of the cranial nerves (often linked intimately to some of the main SWS symptoms), and angiomatosis describes a diffuse proliferation of blood vessels deeply amixed with other tissues.

Types of SWS

Some publications break down SWS into three main subtypes:

  • Type 1: the most common manifestation, consisting of skin and neurological symptoms (such as seizures or mental/physical impairments). Glaucoma may or may not be part of the picture
  • Type 2: consisting of skin symptoms and possibly glaucoma, but with no evidence of neurological involvement
  • Type 3: consisting of neurological involvement, but without skin abnormalities. Glaucoma is usually not present. This type is identified through brain scans and may also be known as the isolated neurological variant

The birthmarks associated with SWS are found mainly on the face and they are often the most notable initial symptom. The stains follow the trigeminal nerve (remember the mouthful name above?), which splits into three divisions:

  • ophthalmic area (upper eyelid and forehead), also referred to as V1
  • maxillary area (cheek and lower eyelid), also referred to as V2
  • mandibular area (lower cheek, lower lip and chin), also referred to as V3

It is thought that when the ophthalmic area is involved the infant is at a greater risk of having Sturge-Weber syndrome.

Triggering cause

Based on the current understanding, SWS is not inherited but originates as a result of a protein malfunction during the early development of the embryo. This misbehavior is linked to a mutation in the GNAQ gene, which occurs randomly after conception for no apparent reason.
This implies at least two things: first, the risk of other siblings being affected is thought to be very low, and second, there‚Äôs nothing to blame the parents for.

Genes are responsible to provide instructions for creating proteins, which in turn play a critical role in many functions of the body. When a mutation of a gene occurs, the protein product may be faulty, inefficient, or absent, thus affecting a variable number of organ systems of the body. The GNAQ gene creates a protein known as Gαq that plays an important role in cell function, including the regulation of blood vessels.
As the first mutated cell keeps growing and dividing, the cells derived from it also present the mutation, while other body's cells do not. This helps to explain why the abnormal blood vessel growth occurs in some parts of the body but not in others.

The specific underlying manner in which the Gαq protein function is disrupted is not fully understood yet. However, the identification of the GNAQ gene mutation will allow researchers to focus on a specific direction to better understand how SWS develops and to explore novel methods in how to treat the disorder. For example, therapies such as drugs that specifically target the proteins and pathways associated with the GNAQ gene.

How to treat

The treatment of SWS is directed toward the specific symptoms that are apparent in each person. Treatment may require the timely and coordinated efforts of a team of specialists. Pediatricians, neurologists, dermatologists, ophthalmologists, and other healthcare professionals may need to systematically and comprehensively plan an affected child‚Äôs treatment.
